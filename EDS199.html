3.2.2 Foundational Approach, Methodology, and Strategies


The instructional framework is based on an Integrated Constructivist Approach, which synthesizes the strengths of Piaget's focus on individual discovery and Vygotsky's focus on social guidance.


A. Foundational Theory and Approach


The approach is Integrated Constructivism, drawing on two primary theories:

Theory Applied Core Concept
Piaget's Constructivism Children actively construct knowledge through self-exploration and direct interaction, testing and adjusting their mental schema.
Vygotsky's Social Constructivism Learning is accelerated through social interaction and guided participation within the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD).

B. Learning Methodology (Context)


The theoretical approach is delivered via a Play-Based Learning methodology.

  • Context: Play serves as the primary context for learning. Through manipulatives (e.g., counters, mats, number cards), children are given the freedom to explore and manipulate concepts.
  • Function: Play provides the concrete, hands-on environment necessary for children to actively construct meaning and engage in shared tasks, translating abstract numeracy into concrete experience.


C. Instructional Strategies (Techniques)


The teacher‚Äôs actions are guided by the overall strategy of Socially Guided Exploration, which serves to facilitate both individual and social learning:

  • Socially Guided Exploration (Overall Strategy): The teacher structures a rich environment and provides intervention only when necessary, balancing individual exploration with intentional guidance.
  • Scaffolding (Tactical Strategy): The teacher uses intentional, responsive support (modelling, prompting questions, cues) to assist the learner in their ZPD.
  • Exploratory Play & Social Interaction (Tactical Strategy):The design allocates time for both:
    • Individual Exploration: Children work independently to test hypotheses (Piaget).
    • Group Interaction: Pair work and guided discussions are used to compare solutions, articulate strategies, and co-construct understanding (Vygotsky).

üß† 3.3 Theoretical Underpinning and Alignment

The design and implementation choices were firmly grounded in established learning theories, maintaining fidelity to the Constructivist philosophy outlined in the Home section.

3.3.1 Rationale for Instructional Design Choices

A. Pedagogical Rationale


The selection of the Integrated Constructivist Approach and Play-Based Learning is paramount because:

  • Holistic Development: This integrated framework addresses both the individual cognitive drive (Piaget) and the social-emotional necessity of learning (Vygotsky). It ensures learning is both personally meaningful and socially enriched.
  • Effective Skill Transfer: The combination ensures the intervention is both intrinsically motivating (Play-Based) and effective (targeted support via Scaffolding in the ZPD ), leading to deeper, more durable learning gains in numeracy.

B. Implementation Rationale


The ADDIE Model was selected to manage the complexity and accountability of the short-term intervention

  • Systematic Quality Control: ADDIE mandates a structured process where the initial Analysis phase directly informs the Design and Development, guaranteeing that the activities are aligned with the learners' specific baseline needs.
  • Accountability and Evaluation: The model requires a formal Evaluation phase. The planning for both pre- and post-assessments ensures that learning gains are systematically tracked, providing empirical evidence of the intervention‚Äôs impact.

3.3.2 Instructional Design Model (ADDIE MODEL)


The ADDIE Model was chosen as the framework for designing, implementing, and evaluating the learning sessions. It consists of five phases: Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, and Evaluation.

  • Analysis: This phase involved identifying the learners‚Äô needs and readiness for the lessons. Insights were gathered through an informal needs assessment conducted by Ma‚Äôam Flordeliza, highlighting the children‚Äôs current skills, learning preferences, and areas for support in number recognition and sequencing (1‚Äì10). A formal pre-assessment will be conducted prior to the first session to establish baseline data, which will guide the planning of lessons and activities.
  • Design: Learning objectives, outcomes, and instructional strategies will be developed based on the analysis. Activities will be structured to be playful, interactive, and developmentally appropriate for children aged 3.5 to 4.5 years.
  • Development: Preparation of learning materials, visual aids, worksheets, and lesson plans is currently in progress. The 10-point numeracy checklist has been finalised and will be used to measure children‚Äôs progress.
  • Implementation: Lessons are planned to be delivered once a week in group sessions every Wednesday. Activities will follow the prepared plans while allowing flexibility to accommodate the pace and needs of the group.
  • Evaluation: Evaluation will be conducted using the pre- and post-assessment only. The pre-assessment will establish the children‚Äôs baseline skills, and the post-assessment will measure learning progress after the sessions.

By integrating the informal needs assessment and planning for a formal pre-assessment, the ADDIE approach ensures a structured and learner-centered design for the upcoming group-based learning sessions.
